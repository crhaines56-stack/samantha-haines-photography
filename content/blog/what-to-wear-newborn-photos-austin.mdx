There's a lot going on in those first days home with a newborn. You're running on no sleep, operating on pure adrenaline, and somehow trying to remember to eat. The last thing you need is a wardrobe crisis the morning of your photos.

Here's the truth: what your family wears matters — but it doesn't have to be complicated. A few intentional choices made *before* the session means you walk into [Vue Studio](https://res.cloudinary.com/du67vy39a/image/upload/v1775364375/shp/homepage/shp/homepage/service-newborn.jpg) relaxed and ready, not scrambling through your closet at 8 AM with a crying newborn in one arm.

This is your complete guide to what to wear for newborn photos in Austin — from mom's postpartum look to what to do with the toddler who's already suspicious of the whole situation.

## Start With a Palette, Not a Matching Set

The biggest mistake families make is trying to match. Everyone in white. Everyone in navy. It reads more like a yacht club than a family portrait — and it puts enormous pressure on finding the exact same shade of anything.

Instead, build a palette. Choose two or three tones that live in the same family — warm neutrals, cool earthy tones, soft blush — and let each person express that palette in their own way. You're going for harmony, not uniformity.

Colors that photograph beautifully in Austin's aesthetic: cream, ivory, warm beige, dusty sage, soft terracotta, pale blush, oat, and taupe. These tones are warm without being harsh, and they put your baby at the center of every frame — which is exactly where they belong.

## What to Wear: Mom

You just gave birth. Your body is doing something remarkable — healing, producing milk, adjusting to an entirely new reality. The last thing you need is an outfit that makes you feel self-conscious.

Loose, flowy silhouettes are your best friend postpartum. A soft wrap dress, a flowy maxi skirt paired with a fitted but stretchy top, or a delicate oversized knit — these are the shapes that photograph with elegance and *feel* good to wear. Avoid anything structured, corseted, or stiff. You'll be seated, you'll be leaning over, you'll be holding your baby close. You want to move freely.

Fabric texture adds visual interest without adding complexity. Linen, chiffon, ribbed cotton, soft knits — these all catch light beautifully and add dimension to the image in a way a flat cotton tee just doesn't.

## What to Wear: Dad

Keep it simple. A well-fitted linen or chambray button-down in a neutral tone — cream, light gray, soft olive — is almost always the right call. Roll the sleeves. Skip the tie unless you genuinely want it.

If he wants to go more casual, a plain, well-fitted crewneck in a neutral works just as well. The goal is clean, not corporate. He should look like himself on a really good day — not like he got dressed up for a job interview.

What to avoid: busy plaid, graphic tees, branded shirts, or anything too stiff. He needs to hold the baby, hug the kids, and sit on the floor without a second thought.

## What to Wear: Siblings

Kids are unpredictable. You already know this. The wardrobe goal for siblings is simple, comfortable, and palette-adjacent.

Pick something from your family's color palette — a soft sage romper for a toddler girl, a cream linen set for a little boy — and don't overthink it. The simpler the outfit, the less there is to argue about before you leave the house.

Avoid anything too precious that you'll stress about getting dirty, anything with scratchy tags or tight waistbands (they *will* tell you about it), and anything that requires extra fussing once you arrive. The session works best when the sibling feels comfortable and unencumbered — which means they're more likely to actually smile.

## What to Avoid, Full Stop

A few things that consistently derail what could be beautiful images:

**Busy patterns.** Florals, plaid, stripes, paisley — your eye goes to the pattern before it goes to the faces. That's the opposite of what you want.

**Logos and text.** Brand names, sports teams, anything with words across the chest. They date the photo and distract from the moment.

**Bright, saturated colors.** A single bright red sweater will dominate every frame it's in. It's not about the color being wrong — it's about the hierarchy. Your baby should be the focal point, always.

**Stiff or formal clothing.** A tuxedo shirt on a 4-year-old is adorable in theory. In a studio, after twenty minutes, it's a negotiation.
